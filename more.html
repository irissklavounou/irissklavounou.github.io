UNEARTHING CRIME & PUNISHMENT IN THOMAS MORE’S IDEAL COMMONWEALTH

In Thomas More’s Utopia, the punitive system in 15th Century England was criticized as being ineffective in deterring crime. An ideal commonwealth could be achieved through legal reform, bringing a curtailment in crime with just and appropriate punishment. My paper uses specific instances of character exchanges to identify the author’s position on the components of crime and punishment in an ideal commonwealth that supports the aforementioned claim. It first explores the ineffectiveness of punishment, and then dives into the overly-complex legislation.

I compiled a thorough biography of Thomas More that is attached in the Powerpoint.
